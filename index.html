Where Part Six Stands in the Series

Part Five closed by specifying three limits that the abhinaya-system's technical account cannot resolve from within its own domain. The first — the cultivation-question: what training practice, over what duration and through what methods, produces the concentrated imaginative orientation bhāvanā requires — belongs to Yoga-śāstra's disciplinary specificity. The second — the collective experience question: how the co-presence of multiple sahṛdayas modifies individual rasāsvādana — finds partial address in the Yoga-śāstric concept of puruṣa as a shared ontological ground. The third — the citta-formation question: what it is to train a mind toward the quality of absorbed, discriminating, affectively open attention that makes Zone Two functioning possible — is precisely Pātañjali's subject-matter in the Yogasūtra, and this paper exists because that subject-matter is the deepest explanatory layer underneath everything Parts Three through Five have developed.

The handoff from Part Five is not merely thematic but structurally determined. Part Five Section 9.3 identified the actor's bhāvanā-practice as structurally parallel to Pātañjali's dhāraṇā — sustained concentration on a single object — and named that parallel as the primary bridge-concept between the aesthetic domain (Parts IV–V) and the yogic domain (Part VI). Part Five Section 7.4's three-zone model (fully voluntary / Zone Two bhāvanā / Zone Three involuntary) maps directly onto Pātañjali's account of the citta's relationship to voluntary and involuntary processes, and the present paper develops that mapping in full. And Part Five Section 11.3 identified the citta-formation question as requiring Yoga-śāstra's disciplinary specificity to resolve — specifying, with deliberate precision, that abhinaya-theory reaches its own outer boundary exactly where Yoga-śāstra's inner subject-matter begins.

The Central Claim of Part Six

This paper's central claim is that Pātañjali's Yogasūtra, read through its principal commentaries (Vyāsa's Bhāṣya, Vācaspati Miśra's Tattvavaiśāradī, Vijñānabhikṣu's Yogavārttika), constitutes not merely a discipline of individual psychological self-regulation but a systematic account of what the citta must become — through the progressive restraint of its habitual vṛtti-patterns and the cultivation of the samāpatti-states — in order to function as the instrument through which the highest forms of discriminative knowledge (viveka-khyāti), aesthetic absorption (rasāsvādana), and imaginative inhabitation (bhāvanā) become possible. Yoga-śāstra is, on this reading, the depth-psychology that Parts Three through Five presupposed without articulating: the theory of the trained, stable, concentrated mind that makes the Sāmavedic priest's tonal discrimination, the classical dancer's sāttvika absorption, and the sahṛdaya spectator's rasa-competence all achievable rather than merely conceivable.

Abstract

This paper develops, across twelve sections, a full philosophical and technical reconstruction of the Pātañjala Yoga-śāstra as the systematic theory of citta-formation that supplies the depth-psychological foundation for the aesthetic and performative capacities Parts Three through Five examined at the level of practice. The paper proceeds as follows. First, it establishes the Yogasūtra's architecture — four pādas, 196 sūtras — and the relationship between Pātañjali's text, Vyāsa's Bhāṣya, and the subsequent commentarial tradition, distinguishing the Yogasūtra's own technical vocabulary from its commentarial elaborations. Second, it develops the citta-concept in full: citta as the compound mental instrument composed of buddhi (discriminative intelligence), ahaṃkāra (the sense of individual selfhood), and manas (the sense-collating faculty), whose relationship to puruṣa (pure consciousness) is the central ontological problem Yoga-śāstra exists to resolve in practice rather than merely in theory. Third, it reconstructs the five vṛtti-categories (pramāṇa, viparyaya, vikalpa, nidrā, smṛti) as the five modes of the citta's ordinary fluctuating activity, whose collective restraint (nirodha) defines yoga in Pātañjali's own opening sūtra. Fourth, it examines the aṣṭāṅga or eight-limbed programme as a progressive citta-formation curriculum, treating each limb's function within the overall progression and its relationship to the limbs preceding and following it. Fifth, it develops the inner triad of the last three limbs — dhāraṇā (sustained concentration), dhyāna (unbroken meditative absorption), and samādhi (the fully absorbed state) — as a phenomenologically progressive sequence whose internal structure the paper maps in detail through the samāpatti-concept. Sixth, it develops the samāpatti-states (savitarka, nirvitarka, savicāra, nirvicāra, sānanda, sāsmitā, and the asamprajñāta beyond them) as a phenomenological ladder of progressive cognitive-attentional refinement, tracing the movement from object-supported attention through conceptually-purified attention to the objectless absorption that approaches the limit of citta-activity altogether. Seventh, it develops the paper's central bridge-argument: the structural identity between the actor's Zone Two bhāvanā-practice (Part Five Sections 7.2–7.4) and Pātañjali's dhāraṇā, establishing that the actor's disciplined imaginative inhabitation of a character's situation is formally a directed dhāraṇā on an imaginatively constructed object, whose success produces a form of dhyāna-level absorption (bhāva-samāveśa) that the Yoga-śāstric framework allows to be located with precision within the broader map of attentional training. Eighth, it develops the Yoga-śāstric account of saṃskāra-formation and dissolution (karmāśaya, vāsanā, saṃskāra-śeṣa) in its relationship to the affective-saṃskāra model Parts Three and Four employed, arguing that the Yoga-śāstric account provides the dynamic mechanism — how saṃskāras are formed, deepened, counteracted, and ultimately dissolved — that the rasa-theory and the Sāmavedic accounts presupposed without specifying. Ninth, it addresses the collective ground question (Part Five Section 11.2) through the Yoga-śāstric concept of puruṣa, developing the argument that puruṣa's characterisation as undivided and unbounded consciousness provides the ontological basis for understanding why multiple sahṛdayas, each individually formed and each undergoing individual rasāsvādana, nevertheless experience a qualitatively shared rasa-event during a single performance — the shared ground being not the psychologically individuated citta but the transpersonal puruṣa that each citta, in its moment of samādhi-adjacent rasāsvādana, approaches. Tenth, it examines the major commentarial traditions (Vyāsa, Vācaspati Miśra, Vijñānabhikṣu, and the modern interpretive tradition from Vivekananda through B.K.S. Iyengar to contemporary philosophical scholarship) as distinct interpretive responses to the same foundational text, with specific attention to their divergences on the puruṣa-citta relationship and its implications for the account of samādhi. Eleventh, it specifies three genuine limits in the Yoga-śāstric account as this series has employed it, preparing the ground for Part Seven's engagement with vyākaraṇa and nyāya as the śāstric disciplines in which the yogically-formed citta's discriminative capacity achieves its most technically refined expression. Twelfth, it executes the handoff to Part Seven.

Reading Note — This paper presupposes Part Five's three-zone model (Section 7.4), the bhāvanā-mechanism (Section 7.2), the concept of bhāva-samāveśa (Section 9.2), and the saṃskāra-model of rasa-competence developed in Part Four (Sections VII–VIII). Readers without Part Four's sādhāraṇīkaraṇa-account and Part Five's Zone Two concept will find Section VII's bridge-argument inaccessible; those sections should be engaged before proceeding to Section VII. The Yoga-śāstric technical vocabulary introduced in Sections II through VI is developed from first principles and does not presuppose prior familiarity with Pātañjali's text, though Vyāsa's Bhāṣya in translation is recommended as a companion to Sections V–VI.

I.

The Yogasūtra's Architecture: Four Pādas, 196 Sūtras

1.1 The Text as Concentrated Technical Doctrine

The Yogasūtra of Patañjali — composed, in the scholarly consensus, somewhere between the second century BCE and the fourth century CE, with the commentarial tradition anchored by Vyāsa's Bhāṣya around the fifth century CE — is one of the most economical technical documents in any philosophical tradition. Its 196 sūtras across four chapters (pādas) are not a philosophical treatise in the sense of an extended argumentative prose composition but a memorisation-and-commentary scaffold: maximally compressed formulations, stripped to the minimal phonemic content required to carry their technical load, whose full meaning is available only in conjunction with an authorised oral commentary transmitted by a qualified teacher. This is a text that could not be understood without a tradition, and whose design assumes a tradition as the condition of its legibility — a structural feature it shares with the Pāṇinian grammar Part Two examined, with the Sāmavedic chant-system Part Three examined, and with the Nāṭyaśāstra's technical chapters Part Five examined: all four are compressed technical manuals whose compression presupposes the living transmission-context that makes their brevity functional rather than merely obscure.

The four pādas organise the text's subject-matter in a progression whose logic this paper treats as deliberate and significant. The Samādhi-pāda (51 sūtras) defines yoga, specifies its goal, and maps the samādhi-states the practice aims at: it is the telos-first exposition, establishing where the discipline leads before describing how it is practiced. The Sādhana-pāda (55 sūtras) specifies the means — the aṣṭāṅga system's first five outer limbs (yama through pratyāhāra) and the theory of kleśas (afflictions) whose reduction these limbs accomplish. The Vibhūti-pāda (55 sūtras) treats the inner three limbs (dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi in their combined operation as saṃyama) and the extraordinary perceptual and cognitive capacities (vibhūtis) their sustained practice makes available — capacities the tradition treats as signs of progress rather than as the practice's aim. The Kaivalya-pāda (34 sūtras) addresses the ultimate telos — kaivalya, the puruṣa's liberation from prakṛti's entanglement — and develops the ontological framework (the Sāṃkhya-derived categories of puruṣa, prakṛti, guṇas) that gives the entire practice its metaphysical orientation and justification.

1.2 Pātañjala Yoga's Relationship to Sāṃkhya

Pātañjala Yoga inherits from the Sāṃkhya philosophical system its basic ontological categories — the fundamental duality of puruṣa (pure unchanging consciousness) and prakṛti (the ever-changing material-psychic ground of all phenomenal existence) — and deploys them as the framework within which the Yoga-śāstric practice's purpose is defined and its progress measured. Puruṣa is pure witnessing awareness, without qualities, without modification, without agency: it neither thinks nor feels nor acts, but is that by which thinking, feeling, and acting are illuminated — the ultimate light-source of all cognitive and affective processes, not itself any of those processes. Prakṛti is the dynamic, three-guṇa totality of all that is not puruṣa: gross matter and subtle mind alike, including the citta's entire range of vṛtti-activities, are modifications of prakṛti rather than of puruṣa. The problem yoga exists to resolve is the beginningless misidentification (avidyā, false knowledge, the root kleśa) in which puruṣa mistakes itself as identical with prakṛti's modifications — in which pure witnessing consciousness believes itself to be the thinker, the feeler, the actor, the one who suffers and who desires. The practice's entire structure is organised around the progressive dissolution of this misidentification through the citta's own increasingly purified self-understanding, culminating in the kaivalya in which puruṣa rests in its own nature, fully distinguished from all prakṛtic modification.

1.3 Why This Ontological Framework Matters for the Series

The puruṣa-prakṛti framework is not merely a metaphysical presupposition that the Yoga-śāstric account carries as philosophical baggage from its Sāṃkhya inheritance; it is the framework within which the aesthetic and psychological accounts this series has developed receive their deepest available theoretical grounding. The rasa-theory's account of sādhāraṇīkaraṇa — the generalisation through which the particular, personally-identified spectator's emotion is transmuted into the universalised, aesthetically-distanced rasa-experience — names, in the Yoga-śāstric framework's terms, a moment in which the spectator's citta, ordinarily occupied with its own vṛtti-driven particular concerns, achieves a temporary reduction of those concerns sufficient for the latent saṃskāra to manifest as a shared affective form. This temporary reduction is structurally analogous to — though less complete than — the nirodha of ordinary vṛtti-activity that yoga's inner limbs progressively deepen toward samādhi. The sahṛdaya's rasāsvādana is, on this reading, a partial and temporarily-achieved approach toward the condition that the yogin's samādhi-practice approaches more fully and more sustainably: the condition of a citta sufficiently quieted that its own content — in the sahṛdaya's case, the universalised sthāyibhāva; in the yogin's case, the puruṣa's own unmodified nature — can become available to awareness without the distortion and fragmentation of ordinary vṛtti-driven cognitive activity.

II.

Citta and Its Structure: The Compound Mental Instrument

2.1 The Sūtra's Opening Definition

योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ Yoga is the restraint of the citta's fluctuations. — YS I.2

Patañjali, Yogasūtra I.2

The Yogasūtra's second sūtra — the definition of yoga, preceded only by the atha yogānuśāsanam ("now, the discipline of yoga") that opens the text — is among the most concentrated technical formulations in any philosophical tradition. Three terms do the definition's entire work: citta (the compound mental instrument), vṛtti (the fluctuation, modification, or turning of that instrument), and nirodha (the restraint, cessation, or channelling of those fluctuations). Understanding yoga requires understanding each of these terms with precision and understanding their relationship with equal precision.

Citta, as Vyāsa's Bhāṣya specifies, is not a simple substance but a compound or layered faculty, comprising three distinguishable but operationally inseparable functions: buddhi (also called mahat, the great principle — the faculty of discriminative intelligence and decisional clarity), ahaṃkāra (the ego-sense, the appropriating function that applies the possessive marker "mine" and the identifying marker "I am this" to all experience), and manas (the sense-collating faculty that receives the raw data of sensory input, coordinates the sense-organs' multiple streams, and presents sorted perceptual objects to buddhi for evaluation and decision). These three together constitute the citta in its functional totality, and their interactions with each other — buddhi evaluating what manas presents; ahaṃkāra appropriating buddhi's evaluations as "my judgments," "my beliefs," "my knowledge" — constitute the vast majority of ordinary human cognitive and affective life.

2.2 The Citta's Relationship to Puruṣa: The Problem of Reflection

Puruṣa — the pure, unchanging, witnessing consciousness — does not interact with the citta in any causal sense (causal interaction would require puruṣa to be modified by the interaction, which contradicts its defining character as unmodifiable). The relationship between puruṣa and citta is instead understood, in the Sāṃkhya-Yoga framework, through the metaphor of reflection: the citta, in its most transparent or sāttvika state (when the rajas and tamas guṇas — the energising-agitating and obscuring-inertialising principles — are minimally operative), reflects puruṣa's light with the fidelity of a still, clear mirror; in its ordinary turbulent state (rajas predominating, vṛtti-activity constant), it reflects puruṣa's light in distorted, flickering, multiply-refracted form, such that the identification of the reflection with the original — taking the flickering citta-modification for puruṣa's own activity — is structurally unavoidable at the level of ordinary cognitive experience. Avidyā, the root kleśa, is precisely this structural unavoidability: not an error anyone makes deliberately, but the default condition of a citta in which the guṇic balance has not yet been shifted toward sufficient sattva-predominance to permit clear reflection and therefore clear discrimination (viveka) between puruṣa and its citta-reflection.

2.3 The Five Kleśas: The Structural Sources of Vṛtti-Distortion

Before specifying the vṛtti-categories themselves, the Yogasūtra identifies five kleśas — afflictions or distorting conditions — that are the structural sources of the citta's problematic vṛtti-production. The five are: avidyā (false knowledge, the root misidentification of puruṣa with prakṛti); asmitā (the ego-sense, the derivative misidentification of the seer with the instrument of seeing); rāga (attraction, the affective pull toward objects that have produced pleasure); dveṣa (aversion, the affective push away from objects that have produced pain); and abhiniveśa (the clinging to existence, the species-wide investment in continued personal survival). These five are not accidental or individually-produced cognitive errors but structural features of the citta's pre-yogic condition, and the aṣṭāṅga programme's successive limbs are organised precisely around their progressive attenuation: the outer limbs (yama through pratyāhāra) address the behavioural and sensory dimensions of kleśa-driven activity; the inner limbs (dhāraṇā through samādhi) address the cognitive and attentional dimensions at their root.

III.

The Five Vṛtti-Categories: A Taxonomy of Mental Fluctuation

3.1 Vṛtti as Modification

Vṛtti — from √vṛt, "to turn, to move, to be active" — names, in the Yoga-śāstric context, any modification or activity of the citta: any turning of the mental instrument into a particular configuration, any actuation of the citta's potential into a specific cognitive act. Vṛttis are not in themselves problematic; they are the citta's mode of functioning, and functioning of some kind is necessary for any cognitive or affective life. What yoga aims at is not the elimination of all citta-activity (which would be cognitive death, not liberation) but the progressive replacement of the kleśa-driven, avidyā-rooted, reactive vṛtti-patterns of ordinary cognition with the increasingly concentrated, increasingly purified, increasingly non-reactive cognitive modes that the inner limbs develop — until, in the highest samādhi-states, even these refined cognitive modes are stilled in the nirodha that is yoga's culmination.

The Yogasūtra identifies five vṛtti-categories, each further subdivided into kleśa-laden (kliṣṭa) and non-kleśa-laden (akliṣṭa) forms — a subdivision that establishes, crucially, that not all instances of each vṛtti-type are equally problematic for the yogic project:

प्रमाण Pramāṇa · Valid Knowing

The three valid means of knowledge: direct perception (pratyakṣa), inference (anumāna), and testimony (āgama). The citta operating in its knowledge-acquiring mode. Not inherently kleśa-laden, but easily appropriated by ahaṃkāra into "my knowledge," generating pride and attachment.

विपर्यय Viparyaya · Misperception

False knowledge whose basis does not match the object: the rope mistaken for a snake; the changeable citta mistaken for unchanging puruṣa. The structural home of avidyā, and the vṛtti-type most directly targeted by viveka-khyāti's progressive development.

विकल्प Vikalpa · Conceptual Construction

Verbal-cognitive construction without corresponding reality: the concept of "the soul's liberation," which names something real, and the concept of "a square circle," which does not. Language's productive capacity to generate objects that do not exist in perception but may guide or mislead cognitive activity. Critical for Part Two's account of śabda-bheda.

निद्रा Nidrā · Sleep

The citta's activity in the absence of ordinary cognitive support — the vṛtti whose content is the experience of absence or blankness itself. Included because even sleep is a citta-modification, whose quality (refreshing / disturbed, deep / shallow) affects the citta's post-sleep capacity for concentration and discrimination.

स्मृति Smṛti · Memory

The retention of past experience without loss of its cognitive form: the saṃskāra's active form as recalled content. The mechanism through which past experience — including all past affective experience — remains operative in the present citta and conditions current perception, judgment, and response. Directly continuous with the saṃskāra-model Parts III and IV employed.

IV.

Aṣṭāṅga: The Eight-Limbed Programme as Progressive Citta-Formation

4.1 The Curriculum Logic of the Eight Limbs

The eight limbs of Pātañjala Yoga are not eight independent practices but a single progressive curriculum in which each limb both requires and prepares the limb that follows it. The outer five limbs — yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra — address the conditions under which concentrated inner work is possible: behavioural patterning (yama and niyama), bodily stability and ease (āsana), prāṇic regulation (prāṇāyāma), and sensory withdrawal (pratyāhāra). The inner three — dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi — are the concentrated inner work itself, and cannot be consistently achieved without the outer five's prior establishment. The progression from outer to inner is not temporal-sequential in the sense of completing the outer limbs and then beginning the inner ones; it is structural-preparatory in the sense that each outer limb creates the conditions for the next limb's increasing concentration to be stable rather than interrupted by the conditions the outer limb addresses.

Yama · Ethical Restraints
The five abstentions: ahiṃsā (non-harming), satya (truthfulness), asteya (non-stealing), brahmacarya (continence), aparigraha (non-possessiveness). Universal in application (mahāvrata — the great vow, binding regardless of class, time, place, or circumstance). Addresses the behavioural dimension of kleśa-driven activity.
Prerequisite: reduces the gross behavioural reinforcement of kleśas that would otherwise constantly disturb the citta's attentional capacity
2
नियम
Niyama · Observances
The five cultivations: śauca (purity of body and mind), santoṣa (contentment), tapas (disciplined effort / austerity), svādhyāya (self-study / recitation of sacred texts), Īśvara-praṇidhāna (dedication to the Lord). Complements yama's restraints with positive cultivations that build the sattvic quality the citta needs for clear reflection.
Builds sattva-predominance; contentment (santoṣa) directly counteracts rāga-dveṣa's perpetual dissatisfaction
3
आसन
Āsana · Posture
A stable, comfortable, sustained bodily position — Pātañjali's own specification is minimal: sthira-sukham āsanam ("posture is [what is] steady and easeful"). The Yogasūtra does not enumerate specific postures; this is a later development of the haṭha-yoga tradition. The functional purpose is the elimination of bodily restlessness as a source of citta-disturbance during the inner limbs' practice.
Removes the body from the list of disturbances: the citta cannot sustain dhāraṇā if the body is demanding attention through discomfort or instability
4
प्राणायाम
Prāṇāyāma · Breath-Regulation
The deliberate regulation of the breath's four phases — pūraka (inhalation), kumbhaka (retention after inhalation), recaka (exhalation), bāhya-kumbhaka (suspension after exhalation) — in terms of duration, continuity, and place of focus. Prāṇāyāma's yogic function is to regulate the prāṇic dimension of citta-activity: the breath's rhythm and the citta's activity-level are, in the Indian physiological model, mutually interdependent, such that deliberate breath-regulation induces a corresponding regulation of the citta's fluctuation-rate.
Acts as the bridge between outer and inner: tataḥ kṣīyate prakāśāvaraṇam — "thereby the covering of the inner light is thinned" (YS II.52)
5
प्रत्याहार
Pratyāhāra · Sensory Withdrawal
The withdrawal of the sense-faculties from their objects — not the cessation of sensory functioning (the eyes remain capable of seeing) but the de-coupling of the citta's attention from sensory input, so that the sense-faculties no longer pull the citta's attention outward automatically. Pratyāhāra is simultaneously the last of the outer limbs and the necessary precondition of the inner ones: without it, dhāraṇā is constantly interrupted by uninvited sensory input.
The hinge: the last outer limb and the gateway to the inner triad
6
धारणा
Dhāraṇā · Sustained Concentration
The binding of the citta to a single locus (deśa) — a specific object, region of the body, concept, image, or mantra — without the habitual drifting to other objects that characterises the pre-pratyāhāra citta. The object of dhāraṇā may be gross (a visible form) or subtle (a concept, a prāṇic centre), internal or external. What constitutes dhāraṇā as a distinct limb is the deliberate and sustained maintenance of the citta's orientation toward the chosen locus against its own habitual drift.
Bridge to Part Five: the actor's bhāvanā is structurally a dhāraṇā on an imaginatively constructed object (see Section VII)
7
ध्यान
Dhyāna · Unbroken Absorption
The continuous, unbroken flow of the citta's cognitive content toward the chosen object — dhāraṇā sustained past the threshold at which the maintenance-effort itself becomes transparent and the citta's orientation toward its object becomes the citta's entire activity. The distinction from dhāraṇā is one of continuity: dhāraṇā still involves discrete returns of attention after brief driftings; dhyāna is the state in which the drift no longer occurs and the citta's activity is fully absorbed in its object.
Corresponds to bhāva-samāveśa (Part V Section 9.2): the actor in deep bhāvanā is in a dhyāna-equivalent absorption in the character's situation
8
समाधि
Samādhi · Complete Absorption
The condition in which the citta's own cognitive form — its awareness of itself as cognising — becomes transparent, and only the object itself shines forth as if without a cognising subject: tad-evārtha-mātra-nirbhāsaṃ svarūpa-śūnyam iva samādhiḥ (YS III.3). The culmination of the inner triad, achieved in progressive stages (the samāpatti-states) from object-supported to objectless.
The condition toward which rasāsvādana is a partial, aesthetically-achieved approach (see Section IX)

V.

Dhāraṇā, Dhyāna, Samādhi: The Inner Triad and Saṃyama

5.1 Saṃyama: The Three Inner Limbs as a Unified Operation

The Vibhūti-pāda introduces the concept of saṃyama — the simultaneous, unified practice of dhāraṇā, dhyāna, and samādhi applied to a single object — as the operative concept governing the inner triad's practical application. Saṃyama is not the sequential performance of dhāraṇā followed by dhyāna followed by samādhi; it is the achievement, on a given object, of the full three-fold depth simultaneously: the object is held in concentrated attention (dhāraṇā), the attention flows without interruption (dhyāna), and the cognitive boundary between object and cogniser becomes transparent (samādhi), all as aspects of a single attentional event rather than as successive stages of a process. The vibhūtis — the extraordinary perceptual and cognitive capacities the Vibhūti-pāda catalogues, from knowledge of past and future to understanding of others' minds to knowledge of the arrangements of the stars — arise, on Pātañjali's account, from the application of saṃyama to appropriate objects: applying saṃyama to the operations of the body yields knowledge of the body; applying saṃyama to the structure of time yields knowledge of past and future; applying saṃyama to the nature of another person's citta yields access to that person's cognitive content.

The vibhūtis are treated in this paper not as the central subject-matter but as diagnostic indicators: they reveal what saṃyama's depth of attentional penetration achieves by cataloguing the kinds of knowledge that become available when the citta's attention achieves that penetration with respect to specific objects. For the series' purposes, the most significant vibhūti-claim is the one that applies saṃyama to the citta itself: saṃyama applied to the citta's own vṛtti-structure produces knowledge of the citta's own nature, which is the prerequisite for the viveka-khyāti (discriminative insight into the distinction between puruṣa and citta) that is the direct antechamber of kaivalya. This self-reflexive application of saṃyama — the citta examining itself with the full depth of its own concentrated attention — is the Yoga-śāstric parallel to the series' recurring theme (established from Part One's treatment of Vāk) of awareness becoming aware of its own ground.

VI.

Samāpatti-States: The Phenomenology of Progressive Absorbed Attention

6.1 What Samāpatti Names

Samāpatti — from sam + ā + √pat, "to fall completely into," "to become fully coincident with" — names the specific phenomenological character of the citta's cognitive mode during the progressive stages of samādhi: the successive ways in which the citta "falls into" its object with increasing degrees of identity, transparency, and depth. The Yogasūtra identifies four principal samāpatti-states in the samprajñāta (with cognitive support) range and the asamprajñāta (without cognitive support) as the horizon beyond them.

सवितर्क Savitarka
The citta absorbed in a gross external object (a form, a sound, a physical sensation) while still maintaining the ordinary conceptual-verbal overlay: the word for the object, the memory of prior encounters with it, and the inferential associations it carries are all simultaneously present in the citta's cognitive content along with the object's direct perceptual form. The most accessible and least refined of the samāpatti-states; the ordinary meditator's first experience of sustained concentration.
↓
निर्वितर्क Nirvitarka
The same gross object, but with the conceptual-verbal overlay progressively cleared: the word, the memory, and the inference have become quiescent, and the citta's cognitive content is the object's own form as it is in itself — no longer mediated through the linguistic and memorial matrix that ordinarily interposes itself between perception and its object. The tradition describes this as the citta shining with the object's own light, without the citta's own light contaminating the perception.
↓
सविचार Savicāra
The citta absorbed in a subtle object — a tanmātra (subtle sense-element), a prāṇic centre, a concept as a formal entity — while still maintaining the ordinary temporal-spatial coordinates and inferential elaborations that ordinary subtle cognition involves. The shift from the vitarka-pair to the vicāra-pair marks the shift of the absorption's object-domain from gross material objects to the subtle dimensions of prakṛti that ordinarily lie below the threshold of direct awareness.
↓
निर्विचार Nirvicāra
Subtle object, conceptual-temporal overlay cleared: the citta's absorption in the subtle object as it is in itself, without the inferential and temporal scaffolding ordinary subtle cognition requires. This is the state the Yogasūtra identifies, at YS I.47, as the threshold of adhyātma-prasāda — the inner clarity, the serene lucidity of the citta's most transparent condition prior to the asamprajñāta. The tradition regards nirvicāra-samāpatti as the highest reliably achievable concentration-state for most practitioners.
↓
सानन्द · सास्मित Sānanda / Sāsmitā
Two further samāpatti-states sometimes identified by Vyāsa's commentary as refinements beyond the nirvicāra: sānanda (absorption accompanied by the quality of ānanda, a bliss whose object is the citta's own sattvic nature) and sāsmitā (absorption accompanied only by the residual sense of "I am," the most attenuated form of ahaṃkāra). These approach the limit of samprajñāta samādhi: absorption still with some cognitive support, but support of the finest, most transparent kind.

असम्प्रज्ञात Asamprajñāta
The objectless absorption: samādhi without cognitive support, the condition in which even the refined residual cognitive activity of sāsmitā has ceased and the citta rests in a condition that is not blank unconsciousness (nidrā) but the cessation of all vṛtti-activity in its ordinary sense — a state whose positive characterisation in terms of ordinary cognitive categories is impossible by definition, since all such characterisations are themselves vṛtti-products. The tradition names this as the direct antechamber of kaivalya and treats its stabilisation as the culminating attainment of the yogic path.

VII.

Bhāvanā and Dhāraṇā: The Bridge from Part Five

7.1 Restating the Bridge-Argument

Part Five Section 9.3 identified the actor's bhāvanā-practice as structurally parallel to Pātañjali's dhāraṇā and named that parallel as the primary bridge-concept between Parts Five and Six. The present section develops that bridge-argument in full, making explicit the structural identity it claims, specifying where identity ends and the two practices diverge, and drawing the implications for the broader theory of citta-formation the series is developing.

The structural identity claim is this: bhāvanā, as Part Five Section 7.2 described it, is the deliberate, sustained, concentrated orientation of the actor's cognitive-imaginative activity toward a specific object — the character's imaginatively constructed situation — maintained over the duration of a scene or role with the aim of producing, through that sustained orientation, the Zone Two sāttvika responses that constitute the scene's most reliable anubhāva-production. Dhāraṇā, as Section IV.6 above described it, is the deliberate, sustained, concentrated orientation of the citta toward a specific locus — a chosen object — maintained against the citta's habitual drift with the aim of progressively deepening the citta's attentional penetration of that object toward the dhyāna-level continuity and the samādhi-level transparency that constitute the inner triad's progressive goal. The structural parallel is precise: both bhāvanā and dhāraṇā involve (a) deliberate initial direction of the citta's attention toward a chosen object; (b) sustained maintenance of that directional orientation against habitual drift; (c) the deepening, through sustained maintenance, of the citta's attentional quality from voluntary concentration through absorbed continuity to the threshold of transparent identity with the object; and (d) the production, through that deepening, of cognitive and affective events (sāttvika responses in the actor's case, viveka-insights in the yogin's case) that do not occur in the ordinary vṛtti-driven citta's spontaneous activity.

7.2 Where the Practices Diverge

Three divergences between bhāvanā and dhāraṇā are significant enough to require explicit statement, both to clarify the bridge-argument's scope and to establish what Part Six can add to Part Five's account beyond merely translating the latter's vocabulary into the former's. First, bhāvanā's object is always a narratively-constructed, imaginatively-populated scenario — the character's situation — rather than a single, simple, non-narrative object of the kind Yoga-śāstra typically employs (a mantra, a prāṇic centre, a deity's form). This narrative complexity means that bhāvanā maintains the citta in a more cognitively elaborated form of concentration than standard dhāraṇā: the scenario's narrative detail must be simultaneously available and simultaneously subordinated to the single affective orientation that the bhāva requires, producing a mode of concentration that is broader in cognitive content but narrower in affective direction than typical dhāraṇā's single-object focus. Second, bhāvanā is directed outward in its communicative purpose — the actor's concentrated absorption must generate behaviours that communicate to an audience — while dhāraṇā is directed inward in its cognitive purpose, with no communicative obligation. This outward-directedness means bhāvanā cannot achieve the full withdrawal from sensory engagement (pratyāhāra) that standard yogic dhāraṇā requires; it operates in a state of active performative engagement with the external world, not withdrawal from it. Third, bhāvanā aims at the Zone Two sāttvika responses as its intermediate goal rather than at viveka-khyāti or kaivalya; its telos is the production of rasa in a sahṛdaya audience rather than the yogin's own liberation from prakṛtic entanglement.

7.3 The Rasa-Experience as Samādhi-Adjacent: Refining the Parallel

Part Four's account of sādhāraṇīkaraṇa established that rasāsvādana involves, on the spectator's side, a temporary suspension of the ordinarily-active egoic identification with one's own personal circumstance — the suspension through which the particular bhāva of the depicted character becomes available, generalised and universalised, to the spectator's citta. In Yoga-śāstric terms, this temporary suspension is precisely a temporary reduction in the ahaṃkāra's activity: the appropriating function that ordinarily applies "mine" and "I am" to all experiential content is temporarily, aesthetically quieted by the performance's absorbing vividness, and in that quieting the latent sthāyibhāva-saṃskāra can manifest not as "my grief" or "my love" but as grief or love as such — the universalised affective form that rasa-theory names. This structure maps onto the savitarka-to-savicāra movement within the samāpatti sequence: the ordinary, pre-rasāsvādana spectator is in a savitarka-equivalent condition (perceiving the performance through the verbal-memorial-inferential overlay of personal identification); the sahṛdaya in full rasāsvādana is in a savicāra or nirvicāra-adjacent condition (perceiving the rasa's universalised form with the ordinary overlay of personal appropriation temporarily cleared). Rasāsvādana is not samādhi — the clearing is temporary, aesthetically-induced, and does not achieve the depth or stability that the yogic inner limbs' sustained practice achieves — but it is samādhi-adjacent in the sense of being structurally of the same kind, approached from the aesthetic rather than the disciplinary direction.

भावना → धारणा → ध्यानम् → भावसमावेश → रसः
bhāvanā → dhāraṇā → dhyāna → bhāva-samāveśa → rasa
The actor's progression maps onto the yogic inner triad: bhāvanā is the actor's mode of dhāraṇā; the unbroken sustained absorption in the character's situation is the actor's mode of dhyāna; bhāva-samāveśa (the complete inward coincidence with the character's bhāva, Part V Section 9.2) is the actor's mode of samādhi — and from this absorption, Zone Two sāttvika responses arise, constituting the anubhāvas through which a sahṛdaya achieves their own samādhi-adjacent rasāsvādana.

VIII.

Saṃskāra in Yoga-Śāstra: Formation, Depth, and Dissolution

8.1 The Dynamic Account of Saṃskāra

Parts Three and Four employed the saṃskāra-concept as a structural feature of the citta's affective history: the traces left by past experience that condition the citta's current response-capacity and constitute the latent "library" from which rasa-contact draws. The Yoga-śāstric account of saṃskāra (treated most systematically in the Kaivalya-pāda) provides what those earlier treatments presupposed without developing: a dynamic account of how saṃskāras are formed, how they deepen, how they can be counteracted, and how they are ultimately dissolved in the condition the tradition calls nirbīja samādhi — the seedless absorption in which even the most subtle saṃskāra-residue has been consumed.

Every vṛtti — every modification of the citta — leaves a saṃskāra: a residual trace or potency that disposes the citta toward future vṛttis of the same type. Saṃskāras are the mechanism of habit in the deepest sense: not merely behavioural habit but cognitive, affective, and perceptual habit — the accumulated weighting of the citta toward certain types of response, certain modes of perception, certain patterns of reaction — that is the product of everything the citta has ever undergone. Saṃskāras and vṛttis are in a circular generative relationship: vṛttis produce saṃskāras, and saṃskāras produce the dispositions that make certain future vṛttis more likely. This circularity is what gives the kleśas their tenacity: the vṛttis driven by avidyā and rāga-dveṣa produce saṃskāras that deepen the citta's disposition toward avidyā-driven and rāga-dveṣa-driven vṛttis, which produce further deepening saṃskāras, in a self-reinforcing cycle that ordinary, undisciplined cognitive activity cannot break because every effort to break it from within the cycle uses the very cognitive instruments the cycle has shaped.

8.2 Pratipakṣa-Bhāvanā: The Cultivation of Counterweights

The Yogasūtra's practical response to the saṃskāra-circularity problem — specified in YS II.33–34 within the discussion of yama — is the method of pratipakṣa-bhāvanā: the deliberate cultivation (bhāvanā — and here the term appears in its full Yoga-śāstric technical sense, which is cognate with but broader than Part Five's performative-technical sense) of the opposite cognitive orientation to whatever kleśa-driven vṛtti is currently active. When aversion (dveṣa) arises toward a specific object, the deliberate cultivation of positive regard or at minimum neutral attention toward that object produces a counter-saṃskāra that begins to reduce the dveṣa-saṃskāra's dominance over the citta's future responses. When violence (hiṃsā) is contemplated, the deliberate cultivation of ahiṃsā-oriented thought produces a counter-saṃskāra in the ahiṃsā-direction. Over time and with sustained practice, the counter-saṃskāras can achieve dominance over the kleśa-saṃskāras — the citta's habitual weighting shifts from kleśa-driven response toward more sāttvic, less reactive response — producing the behavioural and cognitive changes the yama-niyama limbs cultivate at the level of gross conduct and that the inner limbs then address at their subtler cognitive roots.

8.3 Saṃskāra and the Sahṛdaya's Rasa-Competence

The dynamic account of saṃskāra-formation and counter-saṃskāra-cultivation provides the mechanism that the rasa-theory's account of sahṛdaya-formation implicitly required but did not specify. Abhinavagupta's account of the sahṛdaya (Part Four Section VIII) established that rasa-competence depends on a citta enriched by the saṃskāras of wide and deep aesthetic and emotional experience — a citta whose saṃskāra-library is sufficiently rich and sufficiently varied that the performance's vibhāva-anubhāva configuration can precipitate the manifestation of the relevant latent sthāyibhāva-saṃskāra with sufficient vividness for rasāsvādana to occur. The Yoga-śāstric account specifies how that saṃskāra-library is built: through the accumulated vṛtti-traces of genuine aesthetic experience, genuine affective engagement, genuine imaginative inhabitation of situations not one's own — each such experience leaving its saṃskāra, deepening the citta's capacity for the relevant type of response, until the citta has achieved the degree of saṃskāra-richness in the relevant affective domain that makes it a genuine sahṛdaya rather than merely a spectator. Sahṛdaya-formation is, on this analysis, itself a form of yogic cultivation — not the deliberate, disciplined, limb-by-limb cultivation of the aṣṭāṅga system, but the more diffuse and experientially-immersive cultivation that wide, deep, and open aesthetic and emotional experience produces over a lifetime.

IX.

Puruṣa and the Collective Ground of Experience

9.1 The Problem Part Five Left Open

Part Five Section 11.2 identified the untheorised relationship between rasa-theory's individually-pitched account of rasāsvādana and the collective, co-present character of theatrical audience-experience as a genuine gap that Part Five's own account deepened rather than resolved. The specific form of the problem: if each sahṛdaya undergoes their own individual rasāsvādana through their own individual saṃskāra-activation process, what accounts for the qualitatively shared, collectively convergent character of the audience's rasa-experience — the sense in which the rasa-event is not merely N separate individual experiences occurring simultaneously in the same room but something experienced as genuinely common, as a shared event in a shared affective space?

9.2 Puruṣa as Shared Ground

The Yoga-śāstric concept of puruṣa provides the ontological basis for addressing this question, though — and this must be stated with care — the Pātañjala system's own account of puruṣa is, in the commentary tradition's dominant reading (the Sāṃkhya-influenced reading Vyāsa and Vācaspati Miśra develop), a pluralist account: there are many puruṣas, each distinct, each characterised by its own witnessing perspective. On this pluralist reading, puruṣa does not in itself provide the shared ground for which the collective rasāsvādana problem asks; each puruṣa remains its own private witness. However, the monistic readings of the Yoga-śāstric framework — most fully developed in the Kashmir Śaiva tradition, to which Abhinavagupta belongs and whose puruṣa-equivalent (cit, pure consciousness) is explicitly non-pluralist and fully shared — provide the required shared-ground concept. On the Abhinavagupta-reading, the sahṛdayas' individual rasāsvādana events are individual cittas' simultaneous approaches, from the direction of their individual saṃskāra-activations, toward a shared domain of pure affective form — the rasa as universalised, sādhāraṇīkaraṇa-purified affective experience — that is shared not merely as a type (multiple people having similar experiences) but as an event (multiple people's individual experiences converging on a common experiential ground that is the rasa itself, not its individual instances). The shared ground is the aesthetic-affective universalised form that sādhāraṇīkaraṇa produces: not any individual's saṃskāra but the saṃskāra's universalised content, which — precisely because it is universalised, stripped of personal particularity by the aesthetic distancing mechanism — is in principle available as the same object for every sufficiently formed sahṛdaya present.

9.3 The Implications for Practice: Why Collective Performance Matters

The Yoga-śāstric account's resolution of the collective ground problem carries a practical implication that neither rasa-theory alone nor yoga-theory alone makes available: the experience of a genuinely shared rasa-event — multiple sahṛdayas approaching the same universalised affective form from their individual directions — is, for each participant, an experience of their own citta's approach toward the universalised domain of affective form with an external confirmation that the approach has been achieved. The other audience members' visible, audible, recognisable signs of rasāsvādana — their own anubhāvas (the catch of breath, the stillness, the tears, the involuntary vocal responses) — function, for each individual sahṛdaya, as a real-time confirmation that what their own citta is approaching is genuinely the universalised form rather than a merely personal reaction. The collective theatrical space is, on this account, not merely a pragmatic arrangement for the efficient delivery of a performance to many people simultaneously but a mutual self-confirmation system: each audience member's individual rasāsvādana-approach is confirmed, validated, and deepened by the co-presence of others undergoing the same approach from their own individual directions. This is the closest the Indian tradition's theoretical account comes to a sociology of rasa — and it comes to it by way of the ontology of puruṣa, which is to say, by way of precisely the Yoga-śāstric framework Part Six exists to develop.

X.

Major Commentarial Traditions: Divergences and Their Stakes

10.1 Vyāsa's Bhāṣya: The Canonical Interpretive Framework

Vyāsa's Bhāṣya — the earliest surviving commentary on the Yogasūtra, ascribed to a Vyāsa distinct from the Mahābhārata's Vyāsa though the attribution's historicity is debated — establishes the interpretive framework within which all subsequent commentaries operate, whether by elaborating, refining, or departing from it. Vyāsa's most significant contributions to the text's understanding are four. First, the enumeration and definition of the five vṛtti-categories in terms that remain authoritative across the tradition. Second, the specification of the citta's five levels of activity (kṣipta / vikṣipta / mūḍha / ekāgra / niruddha — scattered, distracted, dulled, one-pointed, restrained) as the five stages through which the yogic progression moves, establishing a psychological developmental sequence that maps onto the limbs' progressive curriculum. Third, the development of the samāpatti-states' detailed phenomenology, which the Yogasūtra's own sūtras describe with characteristic brevity and Vyāsa's commentary fills with the experiential specificity that makes the states practically recognisable. Fourth, the identification of Īśvara — the special puruṣa untouched by kleśas, karma, or their results (YS I.24) — as the object of praṇidhāna in the niyama-limb's fifth element, establishing devotional orientation toward Īśvara as a valid and efficacious method of citta-purification alongside the more technically-specified dhāraṇā-dhyāna-samādhi progression.

10.2 Vācaspati Miśra and the Tattvavaiśāradī

Vācaspati Miśra's ninth-century Tattvavaiśāradī — a sub-commentary on Vyāsa's Bhāṣya, and one of the Indian philosophical tradition's most technically precise secondary texts — deepens Vyāsa's framework on two points of particular relevance for this paper. First, Vācaspati develops the distinction between the citta's gross (sthūla) and subtle (sūkṣma) modifications in the context of the samāpatti-states' object-progressions with a precision that clarifies why the vitarka-to-vicāra transition represents a qualitative shift in the samādhi's depth rather than merely a change in its object-type: the vicāra-domain is not simply a different set of objects but a different level of prakṛti's subtle structure, accessible only when the citta's own activity has been sufficiently refined to operate at that subtler level without the grosser vṛtti-patterns that would distort or obscure it. Second, Vācaspati develops the saṃskāra-vāsanā distinction with precision: vāsanās are the more generic, species-wide affective dispositions (toward pleasure and pain, toward self-preservation, toward sensory engagement) that constitute the deepest layer of saṃskāra-residue, while individual saṃskāras are the specific traces of specific past experiences deposited on that generic substrate. This distinction clarifies why the nirbīja samādhi's dissolution of saṃskāras represents a qualitatively different achievement from any ordinary cognitive improvement: it is the dissolution not merely of specific experiential residues but of the vāsanā-substrate that makes saṃskāra-formation of any kind possible.

10.3 The Modern Interpretive Tradition: Continuities and Departures

The tradition of modern Yoga-śāstra interpretation — from Vivekananda's Raja Yoga (1896) through B.K.S. Iyengar's Light on the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali (1993) to the academic philosophical scholarship of Gerald Larson, Edwin Bryant, and David White — represents a substantial interpretive departure from the classical commentarial tradition in several respects relevant for this paper's purposes. Most significantly, the modern tradition has tended to decouple the Yoga-śāstric practice from its Sāṃkhya ontological context — emphasising the experiential and psychological dimensions of the eight limbs while treating the puruṣa-prakṛti framework as optional metaphysical scaffolding rather than as the essential theoretical context within which the practice's goals are defined. This decoupling has made Pātañjala Yoga far more accessible to practitioners from outside the Indian philosophical tradition, but it has come at the cost of precisely the conceptual resources this paper requires: the account of puruṣa as the shared ontological ground (Section IX), the account of kaivalya as the telos whose definition gives all the preceding stages their meaning, and the account of the saṃskāra-dissolution in nirbīja samādhi as qualitatively distinct from all prior attainments. This paper's engagement with the tradition is therefore anchored in the classical commentarial framework rather than the modern interpretive one.

XI.

Limits and the Forward Problem

11.1 The First Limit: Yoga-Śāstra's Individual Orientation

The Yoga-śāstric account, even as supplemented by the Kashmir Śaiva framework's shared-puruṣa ontology, remains fundamentally oriented toward the individual practitioner's liberation as the practice's ultimate telos. The collective aesthetic experience that Part Five identified as untheorised and that Section IX of the present paper partially addressed through the puruṣa-concept is addressed only partially: the shared-ground account explains why multiple sahṛdayas can experience the same universalised rasa-event, but it does not address how the specifically social, institutionalised, and culturally-transmitted dimensions of rasa-competence are produced, maintained, and renewed across generations of practitioners and spectators. For this — for the account of how a tradition of shared saṃskāra-formation is institutionally maintained and transmitted — the series requires the śāstric frameworks of Part VII (vyākaraṇa and nyāya as institutions of discriminative knowledge) and Part X (case studies in transmission), not the Yoga-śāstric account's individually-oriented practice-theory.

11.2 The Second Limit: The Body's Absence

The Pātañjala system's treatment of the body — āsana as the third limb's concern — is notably minimal: the body is addressed as a potential source of disturbance to the inner limbs' practice, and its stabilisation in a comfortable and steady posture is specified as the āsana-limb's sufficient achievement. The elaborate engagement with the body as the primary instrument of expression and communication that Part Five's āṅgika-abhinaya account required — the body not merely stabilised but trained, articulated, and expressive in its every part, the body as the vehicle through which the actor's bhāvanā achieves its outward communicative effect — is entirely outside the Pātañjala framework's scope. This is not a failure of the Yoga-śāstric account within its own domain but a genuine limit of its relevance for the abhinaya problematic: the body's expressive-communicative function requires a somatic framework that the Pātañjala system does not supply and that Part Five had to reconstruct from the Nāṭyaśāstra's own technical chapters. Part Eight's treatment of Āyurveda will address the body from the complementary perspective of somatic intelligence and prāṇic constitution, beginning to supply what the Pātañjala framework leaves unaddressed.

11.3 The Third Limit: Discriminative Knowledge and Its Linguistic Ground

The Yoga-śāstric account of viveka-khyāti — the discriminative insight that is the direct antechamber of kaivalya — treats the instrument of discrimination (the yogically-formed citta in its most refined sattvic state) without addressing the linguistic and logical framework within which discrimination operates at the level of explicit, communicable, teachable knowledge. Viveka-khyāti is a direct perceptual achievement, not a discursive one: it is not the conclusion of an argument but the direct cognitive apprehension of the puruṣa-prakṛti distinction by a citta sufficiently purified to make that apprehension possible. But the tradition of śāstric knowledge — within which Pātañjali's own Yogasūtra operates, and which makes the yogic discrimination communicable, teachable, and institutionally transmissible — is a discursive, linguistic achievement. The relationship between the non-discursive discriminative apprehension that yoga aims at and the discursive, grammatically-structured, logically-organised communicative frameworks within which that apprehension is taught, transmitted, and applied is precisely the relationship that Part Seven's treatment of vyākaraṇa and nyāya will examine: the yogically-formed citta's discriminative capacity meeting the linguistically and logically structured frameworks through which discrimination becomes publicly available knowledge rather than private experiential achievement.

XII.

Forward to Part Seven: From Citta-Formation to Śāstric Discrimination

12.1 What This Paper Has Established

This paper has developed nine principal results. First, it established the Yogasūtra's architecture and its relationship to Sāṃkhya ontology, specifying the puruṣa-prakṛti framework as the essential context within which the practice's goals are defined. Second, it developed the citta-concept in full — buddhi, ahaṃkāra, and manas as the compound instrument; the five kleśas as the structural sources of its distortion; the guṇas as the dynamic principles of its quality. Third, it reconstructed the five vṛtti-categories as the five modes of ordinary citta-activity whose collective restraint defines yoga. Fourth, it examined the aṣṭāṅga system as a progressive citta-formation curriculum, treating each limb's function and its relationship to the limbs it requires and prepares. Fifth, it developed the inner triad (dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi) and the saṃyama-concept, with specific attention to the vibhūtis as diagnostic indicators of saṃyama's depth. Sixth, it mapped the samāpatti-states as a phenomenological ladder of progressive attentional refinement from savitarka to asamprajñāta. Seventh, it developed the paper's central bridge-argument: the structural identity between bhāvanā and dhāraṇā, the mapping of bhāva-samāveśa onto dhyāna-level absorption, and the characterisation of rasāsvādana as samādhi-adjacent in the sense of being structurally of the same kind approached from the aesthetic direction. Eighth, it developed the Yoga-śāstric account of saṃskāra-dynamics — formation, deepening, counter-saṃskāra cultivation, and dissolution — providing the dynamic mechanism the saṃskāra-model Parts Three and Four employed without specifying. Ninth, it addressed the collective ground problem through the puruṣa-concept, identifying the universalised rasa-event's shared character as grounded in the sādhāraṇīkaraṇa-purified affective form that approaches the character of puruṣa's own unbounded awareness.

Preview of Part Seven: Proliferation I — Vyākaraṇa and Nyāya

Part Seven will examine Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī (vyākaraṇa) and the Nyāya-sūtra of Akṣapāda Gautama (nyāya) as the two śāstric disciplines in which the yogically-formed citta's discriminative capacity — the viveka-khyāti the Yoga-śāstric account describes — achieves its most technically refined expression in the domains of linguistic structure and inferential logic respectively. Three contributions from the present paper feed directly into Part Seven's argument. First, Part Six's account of the citta's progressive refinement through the samāpatti-states establishes the kind of citta for which Pāṇini's grammatical metalanguage and Gautama's logical framework are the appropriate discriminative instruments. Second, Part Six's account of the vikalpa-vṛtti — the citta's verbal-constructive mode, capable of generating objects that correspond to no perceptual reality but that can guide or mislead cognitive activity — establishes the specific problem that Pāṇinian grammar's precision and Nyāya's inference-theory exist to address: the risk of vikalpa-products that are internally coherent verbal constructions without extra-linguistic referents, which both disciplines develop elaborate technical resources for identifying and eliminating. Third, Part Six's identification of the discriminative-knowledge limit (Section 11.3) — the gap between viveka-khyāti as non-discursive direct apprehension and śāstric knowledge as discursive, communicable, institutionally-transmissible understanding — establishes the problem that Part Seven exists to address at its root.

The yogin's discipline is not an escape from the world of word and image and performed gesture but the deepening of the instrument — the citta — through which word, image, and gesture are received, discriminated, and understood. A citta that has learned to hold its attention steady knows something about concentration that an untrained citta cannot know; and what it knows is not merely a technical skill but an epistemically significant expansion of what can be perceived, discriminated, and communicated. The Sāmavedic priest, the classical dancer, and the Pātañjala yogin are, in this series' developing argument, not three separate cultural achievements but three articulations of the same fundamental recognition: that the quality of the attending mind determines the quality of what is attended to, and that the deliberate cultivation of attentional quality is therefore the deepest form of knowledge-production available to a human being. A Note on the Lakṣaṇa–Prakriyā–Udāharaṇa–Phala Structure (Sections XXIX–XXXVI)

The eight sections that follow are organised differently from both the original twelve-section paper and the two prior expansion blocks (Sections XIII–XX's topical-subsection method, and Sections XXI–XXVIII's five-dimensional method). Here, each section follows the four-part sequence traditional Sanskrit technical commentary itself most commonly uses when introducing a new topic of inquiry: first stating the topic's precise definition and scope (lakṣaṇa), then specifying the operative technical method or process by which the topic is handled (prakriyā), then grounding that method in a single fully worked example (udāharaṇa), and finally drawing out the result or implication that example yields for the wider argument (phala). This sequence is chosen because it mirrors how the source disciplines themselves typically proceed — definition before method, method before instantiation, instantiation before consequence — rather than imposing an external analytical grid upon them, as the five-dimensional structure deliberately did.

This block also completes a structural debt: Section 1.2 of the original paper named six Vedāṅgas (śikṣā, chandas, vyākaraṇa, nirukta, jyotiṣa, kalpa) but developed only vyākaraṇa at length. Sections XXIX through XXXIII below take up the five remaining Vedāṅgas in turn; Sections XXXIV through XXXVI close the block with the prakriyā-grammar tradition, the kāvya-vyākaraṇa interface, and modern computational Pāṇinian systems.

XXIX.

Śikṣā: The Vedāṅga of Phonetics and Correct Pronunciation

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Śikṣā, the first-listed of the six Vedāṅgas in Section 1.2's enumeration, is the discipline of phonetics: the precise specification of how each Sanskrit phoneme is to be articulated, in what place of articulation (sthāna) and by what articulatory effort (prayatna), and how phonemes combine, lengthen, and modify one another across word- and morpheme-boundaries in connected recitation. Its foundational texts are the Prātiśākhyas — a body of phonetic treatises specific to each individual Vedic recension (śākhā), of which the Ṛk-Prātiśākhya and the Taittirīya-Prātiśākhya are the most extensively studied — supplemented by independent śikṣā-texts such as the Pāṇinīya-Śikṣā attributed to the grammarian Pāṇini himself.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Śikṣā's operative method is an exhaustive articulatory taxonomy: every Sanskrit phoneme is classified by sthāna (the point of articulatory contact — guttural, palatal, retroflex, dental, labial), by prayatna (the manner of articulation — stop, fricative, nasal, and the further distinction of voiced/voiceless and aspirated/unaspirated), and by additional prosodic properties including svara (the three Vedic pitch-accents: udātta, anudātta, svarita) and mātrā (vowel-length, in units of short, long, and prolated duration). This taxonomy is then applied recursively to the full sandhi-system — the rules governing phonetic change at word- and syllable-boundaries — with a precision considerably finer-grained than the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own sandhi-treatment (Section 2.2's example sūtra VI.1.101 is itself a sandhi-rule, but stated at the grammatical rather than the strictly phonetic level of description).

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

अकुहविसर्जनीयानां कण्ठः
Pāṇinīya-Śikṣā, articulatory classification
akuhavisarjanīyānāṃ kaṇṭhaḥ
The throat (kaṇṭha) is the place of articulation for the vowel a, the velar consonants (k, kh, g, gh, ṅ), the phoneme h, and the visarga (the aspirated breath-release marked ḥ). This single śikṣā-statement specifies, with anatomical precision, the shared articulatory point of an entire phonemic class — a finer-grained, physiologically grounded counterpart to the purely formal pratyāhāra-classes the Śiva-sūtras define for grammatical purposes (Section 2.2).

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Śikṣā's articulatory precision is the physiological substrate the entire grammatical and discriminative edifice this paper traces presupposes without itself developing: the Śiva-sūtras' pratyāhāra-classes (Section 2.2) are formally economical groupings of phonemes whose phonological naturalness, as Section 2.2 already noted, is independently confirmed by their recurrence as rule-domains; śikṣā's contribution is to show that this same naturalness is independently grounded a second time, at the physiological level, in the phonemes' shared places and manners of articulation — a third, anatomical layer of confirmation, alongside the formal-economical and the modern-computational (Section XVII) layers, for the claim that the tradition's phonemic classifications track real structural facts about the sound-system rather than arbitrary notational convenience.

XXX.

Chandas: The Vedāṅga of Metre and Its Discriminative Function

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Chandas, the science of Vedic and classical Sanskrit metre, specifies the syllabic count, the pattern of light (laghu) and heavy (guru) syllables, and the permitted variation within each named metrical form — from the Vedic gāyatrī (twenty-four syllables in three lines of eight) and triṣṭubh (forty-four syllables in four lines of eleven) through the far more elaborate classical metres catalogued in Piṅgala's Chandaḥśāstra (a text whose binary-combinatorial method for enumerating possible syllable-patterns is independently celebrated in the history of mathematics as an early precursor of binary numeral systems and combinatorics).

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Chandas's operative method is fundamentally combinatorial: a metre of n syllables, each syllable being either laghu or guru, admits in principle of 2ⁿ possible light-heavy patterns, and Piṅgala's method for systematically enumerating, naming, and cross-referencing all metrically licensed patterns of a given syllable-count proceeds through a recursive binary-classification procedure (the prastāra, or "spreading out," of all possible patterns) that anticipates, by some two millennia, the combinatorial mathematics later developed independently in the West. The discipline's correctness-criterion is strict: a Vedic verse whose syllable-count or light-heavy pattern deviates from its named metre's specification is, on traditional reckoning, metrically defective (the technical term being chandaḥ-bhraṣṭa, "fallen from metre"), regardless of whether its grammatical and semantic content is otherwise impeccable.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

तत्सवितुर्वरेण्यं भर्गो देवस्य धीमहि । धियो यो नः प्रचोदयात् ॥ tat savitur vareṇyaṃ bhargo devasya dhīmahi · dhiyo yo naḥ pracodayāt The Gāyatrī Mantra (Ṛgveda III.62.10), in the gāyatrī metre: three lines (pāda) of eight syllables each, twenty-four syllables in total, with the specific light-heavy pattern the metre's definition requires. The verse's metrical correctness is, for the Vedic reciter, a condition of its ritual efficacy as serious as its grammatical correctness — the two requirements, metrical and grammatical, operating as jointly necessary, neither alone sufficient.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Chandas's strict, independently checkable correctness-criterion — a verse either does or does not satisfy its named metre's syllable-count and light-heavy specification, with no room for the kind of apavāda/utsarga negotiated exception this paper's Section 3.2 describes within grammar proper — supplies a third complete discriminative system, alongside vyākaraṇa's grammatical correctness and nyāya's inferential validity, in which the discriminating citta's task is to determine, without ambiguity, whether a given linguistic production satisfies a precisely specified formal criterion. That this third system's combinatorial method (Piṅgala's binary prastāra) independently anticipates a mathematical structure of permanent and general scientific significance is itself a further instance of this paper's recurring observation (most fully developed in Section XVII) that the tradition's formal-technical apparatus, wherever sufficiently rigorously developed, tends to possess a structural validity exceeding its own original, narrowly defined purpose.

XXXI.

Nirukta: Yāska's Etymological Method and the Limits of Derivation

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Nirukta, the Vedāṅga of etymology, is most fully represented by Yāska's Nirukta (likely composed prior to Pāṇini, making it among the oldest surviving works of systematic Sanskrit linguistic scholarship), a text whose primary purpose is to explain the meaning of obscure or archaic Vedic words by tracing them to a verbal root (dhātu) from which their form and meaning can be shown to derive, thereby rendering otherwise opaque Vedic vocabulary intelligible to later readers for whom the relevant words had ceased to be part of ordinary spoken usage.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Yāska's etymological method proceeds by identifying, for a given obscure word, a plausible verbal root whose own well-understood meaning, combined with a recognised pattern of suffixation or compounding, yields the obscure word's attested or contextually inferred sense — a method Yāska himself explicitly acknowledges to be probabilistic and reconstructive rather than certain, since the tradition's own oldest layer of vocabulary had, even by Yāska's time, partially lost its compositional transparency. Yāska's Nirukta is also notable for preserving the tradition's earliest explicit philosophical debate on whether all nouns are ultimately derivable from verbal roots (the position Yāska himself, following his predecessor Śākaṭāyana, defends) or whether some nouns are semantically primitive and not so derivable (the position attributed to the grammarian Gārgya) — a debate with direct bearing on this paper's Section 5.1 account of whether words or sentences are the more fundamental unit of linguistic meaning.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

अश्वो वहति अश्वा वा वहन्ति
Nirukta, illustrative etymological method (paraphrased)
aśvaḥ vahati aśvā vā vahanti
Yāska's characteristic method derives the word for "horse" (aśva) from the verbal root vah- ("to carry, to draw"), on the grounds that the horse's defining characteristic activity — drawing a chariot — directly motivates its name; the derivation is offered not as a certain etymology in the modern historical-linguistic sense but as a plausible, meaning-illuminating connection between the obscure or archaic noun and an independently intelligible verbal root.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Nirukta's avowedly probabilistic, reconstructive method — Yāska himself repeatedly flags alternative possible derivations rather than insisting on a single certain etymology — supplies a methodologically important counterpoint to the rest of this paper's largely formal-deductive material: not every discriminative judgment the tradition values is, or claims to be, a matter of certain rule-application; etymological reconstruction is itself a disciplined, technically careful, but irreducibly probabilistic exercise of judgment, closer in epistemic character to the Nyāya pramāṇa of upamāna (Section 8.2's comparison-based knowledge) than to the certainty the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own grammatical derivations (Section 2.1) typically claim — confirming that the tradition's overall discriminative apparatus (Section 20.2) accommodates, and explicitly theorises, more than one grade of epistemic certainty depending on the specific technical task at hand.

XXXII.

Jyotiṣa: Astronomical Computation as a Discipline of Ritual Precision

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Jyotiṣa, in its Vedāṅga sense (distinct from, though historically continuous with, the later horoscopic astrology of the same name), is the discipline of astronomical computation required to determine the correct timing of Vedic rituals — the auspicious moments for specific sacrificial acts, the determination of the lunar and solar calendar's intercalation, and the calculation of seasonal markers on which the Vedic ritual calendar depends. Its earliest surviving systematic text is the Vedāṅga-Jyotiṣa of Lagadha (likely composed in the mid-first millennium BCE), which already presupposes a working numerical-astronomical method for tracking lunar months against the solar year.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The Vedāṅga-Jyotiṣa's operative method is arithmetic rather than geometric or trigonometric (the latter, more sophisticated methods developing only in the later, post-Vedic siddhānta-astronomy tradition of Āryabhaṭa and Brahmagupta): it specifies a five-year intercalation-cycle (the yuga) within which the relationship between lunar months, solar days, and the recurring positions of specific nakṣatras (lunar mansions) is tracked through simple ratio-based numerical rules, sufficient for the ritually required purpose of determining auspicious dates without requiring the more demanding observational and computational apparatus later astronomy develops.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

पञ्चसंवत्सरमयं युगम्
pañca-saṃvatsara-mayaṃ yugam
The Vedāṅga-Jyotiṣa's foundational computational unit is the five-year yuga, within which sixty-two lunar synodic months are reckoned against the solar count, yielding a working approximation adequate for the practical ritual purpose of fixing sacrificial dates, though understood by the later siddhānta-astronomers as numerically coarser than fully accurate solar-lunar reconciliation requires.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Jyotiṣa demonstrates, more directly than any other Vedāṅga, that the tradition's discriminative apparatus was never confined to purely linguistic or purely logical subject-matter: the same commitment to precise, rule-governed, technically specified procedure that vyākaraṇa applies to phonemes and nyāya applies to inferential structure, jyotiṣa applies to the observed and computed motions of celestial bodies, for the entirely practical purpose of ensuring ritual correctness in time rather than merely in word or argument — direct evidence, prior to and independent of the applied-śāstra material Part Eight will examine (Arthaśāstra, Āyurveda), that the tradition's discriminative impulse extends as readily to empirical-computational domains as to linguistic-logical ones.

XXXIII.

Kalpa: The Śrauta-Sūtras and the Codification of Ritual Procedure

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Kalpa, the sixth Vedāṅga, is the discipline of ritual procedure: the precise specification, for each major category of Vedic sacrifice, of the sequence of actions, the required materials, the qualified participants, and the correctly recited mantras, codified in the Śrauta-Sūtras (covering the major public Vedic sacrifices) and the Gṛhya-Sūtras (covering domestic life-cycle rites), of which the Śrauta-Sūtras attributed to Āpastamba, Baudhāyana, and Kātyāyana are among the most extensively studied.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The Śrauta-Sūtras' operative method is procedural specification through an ordered sequence of prescribed acts (vidhi), each act's precise timing, manner of execution, and required accompanying recitation being specified with a technical exactitude directly comparable, in its formal character, to the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own rule-sequencing (Section 2.1): a sacrifice, like a grammatically derived word-form, is correctly performed only if every required step occurs in the prescribed order, with the prescribed material and recitational accompaniment, conflicts between competing prescriptions being resolved through interpretive principles substantially continuous with the Mīmāṃsā hermeneutic apparatus already examined in Section XXI, since the Śrauta-Sūtras and the Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra address closely related, partly overlapping subject-matter.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

The Agnihotra, the twice-daily fire-offering that is the simplest and most frequently performed Śrauta ritual, illustrates the kalpa-method's characteristic precision: the Śrauta-Sūtras specify the exact moment of performance (at sunrise and sunset, the precise astronomical determination of which depends directly on jyotiṣa, Section XXXII), the precise quantity and type of offered substance, the celebrant's precise bodily orientation and posture, and the exact verbal formula to be recited at each stage — a ritual sequence whose correct performance, like a grammatically well-formed derivation, admits of no informally tolerated approximation, the tradition holding that a ritual performed with even a minor procedural deviation fails to achieve its intended efficacy regardless of the celebrant's sincerity or intention.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Kalpa's strict procedural correctness-criterion, applied to ritual action rather than to language or inference, completes this paper's demonstration (continued from Sections XXIX through XXXII) that all six Vedāṅgas named in Section 1.2 — śikṣā's phonetic precision, chandas's metrical precision, vyākaraṇa's grammatical precision (this paper's primary subject), nirukta's etymological reconstruction, jyotiṣa's astronomical-computational precision, and kalpa's procedural precision — constitute six parallel, mutually reinforcing applications of a single underlying cultural commitment to discriminative exactness across every technical domain the Vedic tradition's self-perpetuation required, vyākaraṇa and nyāya (the latter arising somewhat later as an independent but closely related discipline, Section VI) being, on this completed picture, neither the first nor the only but simply the most extensively and most philosophically self-reflectively developed instances of that single underlying commitment.

XXXIV.

The Prakriyā-Grammar Tradition: From Aṣṭādhyāyī-Order to Topical Order

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

The prakriyā-grammar tradition, of which Bhaṭṭoji Dīkṣita's Siddhānta-Kaumudī (seventeenth century CE) — already mentioned in Section 19.1 as the standard pedagogical entry-point to Pāṇinian study — is the most influential representative, comprises a distinct later genre of grammatical text that reorganises the entire content of the Aṣṭādhyāyī, sūtra by sūtra, into topically grouped derivational sequences (prakriyā, literally "process" or "procedure"), such that all the rules relevant to, for example, the formation of a particular class of nominal declension or verbal conjugation are presented together in derivational order, rather than scattered across the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own economically ordered but topically dispersed sequence.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The prakriyā-grammarian's method is fundamentally a reorganisation rather than a modification of content: every sūtra of the Aṣṭādhyāyī, along with its relevant vārtikas (Section XIII) and Mahābhāṣya-glosses, is retained in full, but is relocated within the text according to which derivational sequence it functions in, such that a student tracing the derivation of a specific word-form can follow, in a single continuous topical sequence, every rule that applies to that derivation — sacrificing the Aṣṭādhyāyī's extreme anuvṛtti-economy (Section 2.1) for pedagogical transparency, precisely the trade-off Section 19.2 identified as motivating the two-stage traditional pedagogical method.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

The Siddhānta-Kaumudī's treatment of the present-tense verbal paradigm for a given conjugational class gathers, in a single continuous topical block, every Aṣṭādhyāyī sūtra bearing on that paradigm's formation — root-selection rules, augment-insertion rules, personal-ending rules, and the relevant sandhi-rules governing their combination — which in the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own order are distributed across several different adhyāyas, their connection visible only to a reader already capable of tracing the relevant anuvṛtti-chains (Section 2.3) across the intervening sūtras.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The prakriyā-grammar tradition's seventeenth-century emergence, more than two millennia after the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own composition, demonstrates that the discriminative apparatus this paper traces is not static but continues to generate new technical genres in response to newly perceived pedagogical and practical needs — confirming, at the level of the grammatical tradition's own internal history, the same pattern of continuous technical elaboration this paper's Section 28.4 identifies in the modern academic and critical-editorial reception of the tradition: each new generation, finding the prior generation's technical apparatus sound in content but imperfectly suited to its own pedagogical circumstances, develops a new organisational layer without discarding the underlying technical substance, exactly the trans-generational, distributed-discrimination pattern Section 13.5 first identified in the trimuni-vyākaraṇa itself, here shown to extend across the entire subsequent history of the grammatical tradition rather than being confined to its three founding centuries.

XXXV.

The Kāvya-Vyākaraṇa Interface: Poetic Licence and Grammatical Boundary-Testing

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

The kāvya-vyākaraṇa interface concerns the recognised, technically theorised space within which classical Sanskrit poetry (kāvya) is permitted to deviate from strict Aṣṭādhyāyī-correctness for specifically poetic purposes — a phenomenon the tradition itself names and analyses rather than either ignoring or treating as mere error, under the technical category of ārṣa-prayoga ("usage of the sages," extending licence on the model of archaic Vedic forms) and chandasi-vikalpa (metrically motivated optional grammatical forms, several of which the Aṣṭādhyāyī itself explicitly licenses only chandasi, "in metrical/Vedic usage," and not in ordinary prose).

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The Aṣṭādhyāyī's own method for handling this interface is to mark certain rules explicitly with the qualifying domain-restriction chandasi or vede, signalling that the rule's licensed form is available only within metrical or specifically Vedic composition and not within ordinary classical prose usage (bhāṣā) — a domain-restriction device structurally continuous with the adhikāra-mechanism (Section 2.3) that governs ordinary rule-scope, but here used to carve out a recognised exception-domain for poetic and archaic usage rather than to resolve an internal rule-conflict. Later kāvya-critics and commentators, particularly in their analysis of difficult or seemingly irregular forms in canonical poets such as Kālidāsa and Bhāravi, apply this same chandasi/bhāṣā distinction to defend forms that a strict prose-grammarian's reading would flag as erroneous.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

छन्दसि लुपो वा प्रथमा
Illustrative pattern, after Aṣṭādhyāyī's chandasi-licensing rules
chandasi lupo vā prathamā
A representative pattern of the Aṣṭādhyāyī's chandasi-marked rules: in metrical composition, certain case-endings may optionally be elided or replaced where ordinary prose usage would require their full retention — a licensed deviation whose availability is strictly bounded by the chandasi domain-marker, and which a poet's classical commentator must correctly identify as licensed rather than erroneous when it occurs.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The kāvya-vyākaraṇa interface demonstrates that the grammatical tradition's own self-understanding of "correctness" is, from its earliest stratum, more nuanced than a simple binary of grammatical/ungrammatical: the Aṣṭādhyāyī itself builds in a recognised, rule-governed space of licensed poetic deviation, theorised with the same technical precision (the chandasi domain-marker functioning exactly like any other adhikāra-restriction, Section 2.3) the tradition applies to ordinary prose correctness — direct textual evidence, internal to vyākaraṇa itself, against any reading of this paper's account of grammatical discrimination (Sections II–III) as committed to a rigid, context-insensitive notion of correctness, and further confirmation of this paper's recurring theme that the specific (here, the poetic exception) operates within and refines the general (ordinary prose correctness) rather than simply violating it.

XXXVI.

Modern Computational Pāṇinian Systems: From Manuscript to Parser

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Modern computational Pāṇinian systems comprise the body of contemporary software — Sanskrit morphological analysers, automatic sandhi-splitters, and full Aṣṭādhyāyī-based derivation engines, several developed within Sanskrit computational-linguistics research groups in India, Europe, and North America — that implement, in executable program code, the Aṣṭādhyāyī's actual rule-system (or a close formal reconstruction of it) in order to automatically generate, parse, or verify Sanskrit word-forms, extending Section XVII's account of structural resonance between Pāṇinian grammar and generative linguistics from the level of comparative analogy to the level of literal, operational implementation.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

These systems' operative method typically encodes the Aṣṭādhyāyī's sūtras, or a derivationally equivalent rule-set, as a directly executable rule-application engine: given a root and a desired grammatical category (tense, mood, case, number), the system applies the relevant chain of sūtras — tracking anuvṛtti-inheritance (Section 2.3), checking adhikāra-domains, and resolving apavāda/utsarga conflicts (Section 3.2) — automatically, in the same logical sequence a human grammarian would follow, and outputs the derived word-form together with, in the more pedagogically oriented systems, a full trace of every sūtra invoked at each derivational step.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

A representative such system, given the root bhū- ("to be") and the specification "third person, singular, present tense, active voice," will automatically apply the relevant verbal-stem formation rule, the relevant personal-ending selection rule, and any triggered sandhi-rules, to output the correctly derived form bhavati, together with an explicit derivational trace listing each Aṣṭādhyāyī sūtra number invoked in the correct order — a fully mechanised reproduction, verifiable against the traditional grammarian's own hand-worked derivation, of exactly the multi-step, simultaneously-tracked rule-application process Section 2.3 describes as the grammarian's central citta-requirement.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

That the Aṣṭādhyāyī's rule-system can be fully mechanised — implemented as an executable computer program that reliably reproduces correct traditional derivations without requiring the implementing system to possess anything resembling a trained citta — provides the sharpest possible empirical confirmation of the precise boundary this paper's Section 17.3 draws between formal rule-application and pratibhā: the mechanisable component of grammatical competence (correct, rule-governed derivation of well-formed strings) is, by direct demonstration, fully separable from whatever further capacity is required for understanding a derived form's meaning as a holistically grasped, immediately apprehended content — confirming that this paper's distinction between the rule-system's formal architecture and the citta's apprehending function is not merely a philosophical thesis resistant to empirical test, but a distinction now directly visible in the working difference between what a Pāṇinian parser can and cannot, even in principle, be built to do.

XXXVII.

Triguṇa: Sattva, Rajas, Tamas as the Citta's Material Constitution

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

The triguṇa theory — inherited, as Section 1.2 noted, from Sāṃkhya — holds that all of prakṛti, including the citta itself, is constituted at every level by three co-present strands or qualities (guṇa): sattva (lucidity, clarity, equilibrium), rajas (activity, agitation, outward-turning energy), and tamas (inertia, obscuration, downward-turning resistance). No prakṛtic entity is ever purely one guṇa; the three are always co-present in shifting proportion, and what changes from state to state — sleeping citta to waking citta, distracted citta to concentrated citta — is not the presence or absence of a guṇa but its relative predominance over the other two.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The guṇa-theory's operative function within Yoga-śāstra is diagnostic and prescriptive at once: Vyāsa's five-level citta-typology (kṣipta, vikṣipta, mūḍha, ekāgra, niruddha — already introduced in Section 10.1) is itself a guṇa-typology in functional disguise. Kṣipta (scattered) and vikṣipta (distracted) citta-states are rajas-predominant; mūḍha (dulled) is tamas-predominant; ekāgra (one-pointed) and niruddha (restrained) are progressively sattva-predominant. The entire aṣṭāṅga curriculum, read through this lens, is a guṇa-management technology: yama and niyama directly target rajasic and tamasic behavioural patterns; āsana stabilises the body's tamasic inertia without inducing rajasic restlessness; prāṇāyāma and pratyāhāra reduce rajas's outward pull; and the inner triad cultivates and then, at the threshold of asamprajñāta, transcends even sattva itself — since sattva, however refined, remains a guṇa and therefore remains prakṛti, not puruṣa.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

सत्त्वशुद्धिः सौमनस्यम् ऐकाग्र्यम् इन्द्रियजयः आत्मदर्शनयोग्यत्वं च
sattva-śuddhiḥ saumanasyam aikāgryam indriyajayaḥ ātma-darśana-yogyatvaṃ ca
Bhagavad Gītā XVIII.37 and the broader Sāṃkhya-Yoga tradition trace a single causal chain: purification of sattva (sattva-śuddhi) yields cheerfulness of mind (saumanasya), which yields one-pointedness (aikāgrya), which yields mastery of the senses (indriya-jaya), which yields fitness for self-realisation (ātma-darśana-yogyatva). This chain is the guṇa-theory's own internal account of why the aṣṭāṅga curriculum's outer-to-inner progression (Section IV) has the specific sequence it has: each stage is a precondition because each stage produces the sattvic increment the next stage requires.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The guṇa-theory supplies the missing material-causal account beneath Section 2.2's reflection-metaphor: the citta reflects puruṣa with distortion not as a brute, unexplained fact but because rajas and tamas are, by their very nature as agitation and obscuration respectively, optically distorting and occluding qualities, while sattva's lucidity is, by its very nature, optically transparent. The entire yogic project can therefore be restated in a single guṇa-theoretic sentence: nirodha is nothing other than the progressive, curriculum-guided shift of the citta's guṇic balance toward maximal sattva-predominance, short of which no degree of effort or technique substitutes, and beyond which (in the guṇātīta state the Gītā names, Section XLI below) even sattva's own subtle binding is finally released.

XXXVIII.

Kriyā-Yoga: The Sādhana-Pāda's Practical Opening Triad

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Kriyā-yoga — "yoga of action," not to be confused with the twentieth-century devotional movement of the same name founded by Lahiri Mahasaya and popularised by Paramahansa Yogananda, though the later movement draws its name from this sūtra — is Pātañjali's own term (YS II.1) for a three-element practical discipline he places at the very opening of the Sādhana-pāda, prior to and partly overlapping with the niyama-limb proper: tapas (disciplined effort/austerity), svādhyāya (self-study, recitation of sacred texts), and Īśvara-praṇidhāna (dedication to the Lord) — the same three elements that reappear, not coincidentally, as the final three of the five niyamas (Section IV, rung 2).

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Pātañjali specifies kriyā-yoga's function directly: samādhi-bhāvanārthaḥ kleśa-tanū-karaṇārthaś ca — "for the purpose of cultivating samādhi and for the purpose of attenuating the kleśas" (YS II.2). This double purpose is significant: kriyā-yoga is not presented as a preliminary stage to be completed before the aṣṭāṅga proper begins but as a continuous, parallel discipline whose function is twofold throughout the entire practice — directly weakening the five kleśas (Section 2.3) at the behavioural and attitudinal level while simultaneously, and by the same operations, preparing the citta's sattvic capacity for samādhi. Tapas burns through tamasic resistance via disciplined effort; svādhyāya counters avidyā through sustained engagement with authoritative teaching; Īśvara-praṇidhāna counters asmitā by redirecting the ego-sense's natural orientation toward a reference point beyond itself.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

Vyāsa's Bhāṣya on YS II.1 illustrates kriyā-yoga's twofold function with the case of a practitioner whose meditation is repeatedly disrupted by restlessness traceable to undisciplined sensory habits: tapas (a fixed regimen of disciplined conduct, sustained regardless of momentary inclination) directly reduces the rajasic agitation generating the restlessness, while the same disciplined regularity, sustained over time, deposits the kind of steady sattvic saṃskāra (Section VIII) on which dhāraṇā's later stability depends — the practical discipline and the deep psychological preparation being, on Vyāsa's reading, two descriptions of a single operative process rather than two separate achievements.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Kriyā-yoga's placement at the Sādhana-pāda's opening — before the aṣṭāṅga's own eight limbs are even named — establishes that Pātañjali does not regard the inner triad's eventual depth as separable from a sustained, parallel discipline of effort, study, and devotional orientation maintained throughout the entire practice. This bears directly on Section 7.2's account of bhāvanā's outward-directed, performatively engaged character: just as the actor's bhāvanā cannot achieve full pratyāhāra-style withdrawal, the practitioner's progress toward the inner triad is never achieved by inner cultivation alone but is continuously dependent on the parallel, outward-facing kriyā-yoga discipline — a structural parallel the original paper's Section VII did not draw but which kriyā-yoga's own placement in the text makes available.

XXXIX.

Karmāśaya and the Kleśa-Karma Relationship

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Karmāśaya — the "receptacle" or "reservoir" of karma — names, in the Kaivalya-pāda's technical vocabulary, the accumulated body of past action-residues that determine the timing, quality, and experiential character (jāti, āyus, bhoga — birth, lifespan, and experience, YS II.13) of future existence. The Yogasūtra's treatment is distinctive in explicitly subordinating karma-theory to kleśa-theory: karmāśaya is rooted in the kleśas (kleśa-mūlaḥ karmāśayaḥ, YS II.12), meaning that action generates binding karmic residue specifically insofar as it is performed under the influence of avidyā, asmitā, rāga, dveṣa, and abhiniveśa, rather than action as such being inherently karma-generating.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

This subordination has a precise practical implication that the Yogasūtra develops with care: an action performed by a citta in which the kleśas have been sufficiently attenuated — through the aṣṭāṅga discipline's progressive operation — does not deposit the same binding karmāśaya that the identical action, externally considered, would deposit if performed by a kleśa-driven citta. The operative criterion for karmic bondage is therefore not behavioural but psychological: the citta's internal condition at the moment of action, specifically its degree of kleśa-freedom, determines whether the action binds or, in the limiting case of the fully kleśa-free citta's action, leaves no residue at all (the condition the tradition associates with the jīvanmukta, the liberated-while-living).

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

सति मूले तद्विपाको जात्यायुर्भोगाः sati mūle tad-vipāko jāty-āyur-bhogāḥ "As long as the root (the kleśas) exists, its fruition is birth, lifespan, and experience." (YS II.13) — Pātañjali states explicitly that karmic fruition's very existence is conditional on the kleśa-root's persistence, not merely modulated by it; the implication the commentators draw is that a citta in which the kleśa-root has been fully excised (the asamprajñāta-stabilised citta of Section VI) generates no further karmāśaya whatsoever, action under such conditions being, in the technical phrase later Vedānta literature borrows from this context, "burnt seeds" (dagdha-bīja) incapable of future sprouting.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The kleśa-rootedness of karmāśaya supplies the precise mechanism connecting Section VIII's saṃskāra-dynamics to the Kaivalya-pāda's soteriology: pratipakṣa-bhāvanā's counter-saṃskāra cultivation (Section 8.2) is not merely a technique for improving present cognitive-affective quality but is, on this fuller reading, simultaneously a technique for progressively reducing the binding-capacity of all subsequent action — the same disciplined cultivation that produces the sahṛdaya's rasa-competence (Section 8.3) is, in its yogic application, the very operation by which karmic bondage is itself attenuated, confirming once more this paper's recurring claim that the aesthetic and the strictly soteriological domains share a single underlying citta-mechanism, differing in degree and orientation rather than in fundamental kind.

XL.

Īśvara-Praṇidhāna as an Independent Path to Samādhi

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Īśvara-praṇidhāna — already introduced as the fifth niyama (Section IV, rung 2) and as the third element of kriyā-yoga (Section XXXVIII) — receives, in the Samādhi-pāda, a further and more striking specification: Pātañjali presents devotional surrender to Īśvara not merely as one supporting practice among the niyamas but as a sufficient independent route to samādhi in its own right, distinct from and parallel to the effortful aṣṭāṅga progression (samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt, YS I.23 — "samādhi is attained, alternatively, through dedication to Īśvara").

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Pātañjali's Īśvara is carefully and narrowly defined (YS I.24–26) as a special puruṣa untouched by kleśas, by karma, by the fruits of karma, or by the karmāśaya (puruṣa-viśeṣaḥ kleśa-karma-vipāka-āśayaiḥ aparāmṛṣṭaḥ) — that is, as a puruṣa already and eternally in the condition the practitioner's own discipline labours to approach, functioning within the system as a kind of limiting case or ideal exemplar rather than as a creator-deity in the theistic sense. The operative method Īśvara-praṇidhāna prescribes is the citta's sustained orientation of devotional attention (praṇidhāna, a term itself closely related to dhāraṇā's "binding" of attention) toward this exemplar, the recitation of the praṇava (the syllable Oṃ, identified at YS I.27 as Īśvara's vācaka, "expressive designation") functioning as the specific technical support for this orientation.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

तज्जपस्तदर्थभावनम्
taj-japas tad-artha-bhāvanam
YS I.28: "Its [the praṇava's] repetition, and the contemplative cultivation (bhāvanam) of its meaning." Note the technical term bhāvanam recurring here in close proximity to its appearance in pratipakṣa-bhāvanā (Section 8.2) and in the actor's bhāvanā (Part Five, Section VII above): the same root cognitive operation — sustained, deliberate, meaning-directed cultivation of a chosen content — is what the practice of mantra-repetition, the practice of counter-saṃskāra formation, and the actor's imaginative inhabitation of a role all, at the level of underlying citta-mechanism, share.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

That Pātañjali offers Īśvara-praṇidhāna as a route to samādhi independent of the full aṣṭāṅga progression has a significant implication for this paper's overall account of citta-formation: the system itself does not present the eight-limbed curriculum (Section IV) as the only viable path to the inner triad's depth, but as the systematically elaborated, effort-based path among at least two the text explicitly recognises — the other being a devotionally-grounded path whose technical mechanism (sustained bhāvanā directed at a single exemplary object) is structurally a form of dhāraṇā in its own right, differing from the aṣṭāṅga's progression chiefly in requiring no prior outer-limb scaffolding. This bears on Section 11.1's identification of Yoga-śāstra's individually-oriented limit: Īśvara-praṇidhāna's communal, devotional, often collectively practiced character (śarīra recitation, communal chanting) provides a partial corrective internal to the Yoga-śāstric tradition itself, prior to and independent of the resources Part VII supplies.

XLI.

The Bhagavad Gītā's Plural Yogas Against Pātañjala Unity

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

The Bhagavad Gītā — composed prior to the Yogasūtra's likely compilation and drawing on an older, less systematised yoga-vocabulary — presents not a single unified discipline but several distinct paths each separately termed "yoga": karma-yoga (the yoga of disciplined, non-attached action), jñāna-yoga (the yoga of discriminative knowledge), bhakti-yoga (the yoga of devotion), and a form of dhyāna-yoga (Gītā VI) whose technical description of meditative posture, breath, and single-pointed attention closely anticipates, without exactly matching, Pātañjali's later, more systematised dhāraṇā-dhyāna-samādhi sequence.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

Where Pātañjali's system is cumulative and progressive — each limb a precondition for the next, the whole curriculum converging on a single telos (kaivalya) — the Gītā's method is, by contrast, explicitly pluralist and adapted to the practitioner's constitutional disposition (Gītā XVIII.41–48 on the guṇa-determined duties of different classes of person): karma-yoga is recommended for the actively disposed, jñāna-yoga for the contemplatively disposed, bhakti-yoga for the devotionally disposed, the text repeatedly affirming (Gītā IV.11, XII.1–5) that these are not competing methods of differing value but genuinely alternative, individually sufficient routes converging on the same ultimate liberation, the choice among them properly determined by the practitioner's own guṇa-constitution (Section XXXVII) rather than by any single method's intrinsic superiority.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

योगस्थः कुरु कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा धनञ्जय yoga-sthaḥ kuru karmāṇi saṅgaṃ tyaktvā dhanañjaya "Established in yoga, perform actions, having abandoned attachment, O Dhanañjaya." (Gītā II.48) — Here "yoga" itself is redefined at II.50 as samatvaṃ yoga ucyate, "evenness of mind is called yoga" — a definition operating at a level of generality that subsumes, rather than specifies, any single technical method, in marked contrast to Pātañjali's own opening definitional sūtra (Section 2.1), which specifies a single precise psychological operation (vṛtti-nirodha) as yoga's defining content.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The contrast between the Gītā's pluralist, disposition-adapted yoga-vocabulary and Pātañjali's unified, cumulative system clarifies something important about how this paper's own central bridge-argument (Section VII) should be read: the structural identity claimed between bhāvanā and dhāraṇā is a claim about the Pātañjala system specifically, not about "yoga" as a generic category encompassing the Gītā's broader usage. Read against the Gītā's own pluralism, the actor's bhāvanā-practice might equally be characterised, in Gītā-vocabulary, as a form of disciplined, non-attached, absorbed action — a karma-yoga inflected with jñāna-yoga's discriminative awareness of the constructed, non-literal status of the imaginatively inhabited situation — confirming that the series' bridge-concept operates at the more technically precise Pātañjala level of description while remaining compatible with, and partially illuminated by, the older and more pluralist Gītā-vocabulary from which Pātañjali's own systematisation in part draws.

XLII.

Haṭha Yoga's Technical Elaboration of Āsana and Prāṇāyāma

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Haṭha Yoga — systematised considerably later than the Yogasūtra, its classical manuals (the Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā of Svātmārāma, c. fifteenth century CE; the Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā; the Śiva Saṃhitā) dating from roughly the same broad period as Vācaspati Miśra's commentarial work and after — takes the bare two-element specification of āsana and prāṇāyāma that the Yogasūtra leaves minimal (Section IV, rungs 3–4) and develops it into an elaborate, independently systematised technical discipline: dozens of named āsanas, detailed prāṇāyāma typologies (the Pradīpikā alone enumerates eight major prāṇāyāma-types), and the additional technical apparatus of mudrās (psychophysical "seals"), bandhas (internal locks), and a subtle-body physiology of nāḍīs (channels) and cakras absent from the Yogasūtra's own vocabulary.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The Haṭha tradition's own stated relationship to Pātañjala Yoga is explicitly instrumental and preparatory rather than competing: the Pradīpikā's opening verses (HYP I.1–2) present haṭha-practice as a ladder (sopāna) to rāja-yoga — the tradition's own term for the Pātañjala system's dhāraṇā-dhyāna-samādhi summit — making explicit what the Yogasūtra's own minimal āsana-prāṇāyāma treatment (Section 11.2 already noted the body's relative absence from Pātañjali's own text) leaves merely implicit: that a far more elaborate and independently demanding bodily and prāṇic discipline can be, and historically was, developed as a technical substructure beneath the outer limbs the Yogasūtra itself names only schematically.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

The Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā's treatment of nāḍī-śodhana (channel-purification prāṇāyāma) specifies a precise alternating-nostril breathing technique, with prescribed ratios between inhalation, retention, and exhalation duration, whose explicit purpose is stated as the purification of the suṣumnā-nāḍī sufficiently to permit the awakening and ascent of kuṇḍalinī — a goal and a technical vocabulary entirely absent from the Yogasūtra's own four-phase prāṇāyāma specification (Section IV, rung 4), illustrating concretely how far the later tradition's elaboration outstrips, in sheer technical detail, the comparatively austere root text.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

Haṭha Yoga's elaboration partially answers, though it does not fully resolve, the second limit Section 11.2 identified — the body's near-absence from the Pātañjala system proper. Where Section 11.2 looked forward to Part Eight's Āyurvedic treatment of somatic intelligence as the resource that would eventually address this gap, the Haṭha tradition shows that the gap was in fact addressed within the broader Yoga-śāstric tradition itself, considerably earlier than the modern reception history (Section 10.3) and through a technical lineage continuous with, rather than external to, the Pātañjala system's own self-understanding — a qualification this paper's original Section 11.2 should be read alongside, since the limit identified there is a limit of the Yogasūtra as a specific text, not of "Yoga-śāstra" as the broader, multi-textual tradition the title of this paper names.

XLIII.

Tantric and Kashmir Śaiva Re-Readings of Samādhi

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

Section IX already introduced the Kashmir Śaiva tradition's monistic re-reading of puruṣa as cit, drawing on Abhinavagupta's framework to resolve the collective-rasāsvādana problem. The present section develops that re-reading's further implications for the samādhi-concept itself: where the classical Pātañjala-Sāṃkhya reading treats samādhi as the progressive stilling of citta-activity in order to expose puruṣa's already-present, unchanging witnessing nature (a recovery-model, since puruṣa was never actually obscured, only misidentified), the non-dual Śaiva tradition — developing concepts found already in the earlier Śaiva and Śākta tantric corpus the Mālinīvijayottara and related texts represent — reframes the entire citta-vṛtti complex, including its most ordinary and turbulent fluctuations, as itself nothing other than Śiva's own dynamic self-expression (spanda), such that samādhi is reframed not as escape from vṛtti-activity into a vṛtti-free witnessing but as the direct recognition (pratyabhijñā, "re-cognition") of vṛtti-activity's own already-divine nature.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

This reframing has a precise technical consequence for practice: where the Pātañjala system's nirodha-method works by progressively reducing vṛtti-activity toward the asamprajñāta limit (Section VI), several Tantric and Śaiva technical lineages — most explicitly the krama and spanda traditions associated with the broader Kashmir Śaiva corpus — develop instead a recognition-method in which ordinary vṛtti-activity, including intense sensory and emotional experience, is not suppressed but directly engaged as the very locus in which Śiva-nature is to be recognised, the technical term udyama ("upsurge") naming the sudden, recognition-based access to this nature that does not require nirodha's gradual, cumulative reduction of citta-activity at all.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

The aesthetic implication of this difference is significant for the series' own argument: where Section 7.3's account of rasāsvādana as "samādhi-adjacent" relies on the Pātañjala-style reduction-model (rasāsvādana approaches samādhi by reducing, temporarily, the ahaṃkāra's ordinary appropriative activity), the recognition-model available within Abhinavagupta's own broader Śaiva framework offers an alternative and arguably more direct account: rasāsvādana need not be read as an approach toward a state in which ordinary affective vṛtti-activity is reduced, but as a direct recognition-event in which the spectator's already-present, ordinarily obscured cit-nature is recognised precisely through, rather than despite, the heightened affective vṛtti-activity the performance induces — a reading consonant with Abhinavagupta's own explicit characterisation of rasāsvādana as camatkāra (aesthetic wonderment, a term he applies elsewhere directly to spiritual realisation itself).

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

The recognition-model available within Abhinavagupta's broader corpus refines, without overturning, Section 7.3's reduction-model account: the two models are not strictly incompatible, since a reduction of ahaṃkāra's ordinary appropriative grip is plausibly a necessary condition for a recognition-event to register as such, but they assign different explanatory weight to the affective intensity of the rasa-experience itself — the reduction-model treating that intensity as something the citta moves through en route to a quieter universalised condition, the recognition-model treating that very intensity as itself already the locus of the recognition. Future work in this series, particularly any treatment of Abhinavagupta's Tantrāloka in its own right rather than only as cited support for the puruṣa-argument of Section IX, should specify which of these two models — or what combination of them — the series' own bridge-argument (Section VII) is ultimately committed to.

XLIV.

Contemporary Neuroscience and the Samāpatti-Ladder: An Empirical Check

I. Lakṣaṇa — Definition and Scope

A substantial contemporary neuroscience and contemplative-science literature — developed since roughly the early 2000s, much of it associated with EEG and fMRI studies of experienced meditators across Buddhist, Hindu, and secular mindfulness traditions — investigates the neurophysiological correlates of sustained meditative attention, providing an external, empirically tractable body of evidence against which the samāpatti-ladder's claimed phenomenological progression (Section VI) can be, with appropriate caution, compared.

II. Prakriyā — The Operative Method

The relevant research method typically combines continuous EEG recording (tracking changes in gamma-band synchrony, alpha and theta power, and default-mode-network activity) with experienced practitioners' own first-person phenomenological reports, correlating measured neural changes with self-reported shifts in attentional quality — a method whose general logic (correlating an external, third-person measurable signature with an internally reported, first-person qualitative state) is structurally analogous to, though methodologically far more constrained than, the Yoga-śāstric tradition's own reliance on a trained teacher's recognition of a practitioner's reported experience as a check on genuine progress through the samāpatti-stages.

III. Udāharaṇa — A Worked Example

Several studies of long-term meditators (using protocols broadly comparable across the Tibetan Buddhist, Theravāda vipassanā, and secular mindfulness traditions most often studied, rather than specifically Pātañjala practitioners, who remain comparatively understudied as a distinct research population) report a reduction in default-mode-network activity — the network associated with self-referential, narrative, "mind-wandering" cognition — correlating with practitioners' own reports of reduced habitual self-referential thought during sustained concentration, a finding that bears a suggestive, though not exact, correspondence to the citta's progressive reduction of ahaṃkāra-driven appropriative activity the savitarka-to-nirvicāra progression (Section VI) describes in purely phenomenological terms.

IV. Phala — Resulting Implication

This correspondence should be stated with the methodological caution the comparison genuinely requires: the neuroscience literature studies populations, traditions, and practice-protocols that are not identical to the Pātañjala system specifically, the asamprajñāta and kaivalya states in particular have no settled neuroscientific operationalisation at all, and the entire research programme remains, as of this paper's writing, at an early and contested stage with respect to the deepest claimed states. What the correspondence does responsibly support is a modest but genuine claim: the broad direction of the samāpatti-ladder's progression — from effortful, narratively-elaborated attention toward increasingly stable, increasingly non-self-referential absorption — finds independent, convergent support from a research tradition entirely outside the Sanskrit commentarial lineage, which is some confirmation, external to the tradition's own authority-structure, that the phenomenological map Vyāsa's Bhāṣya and Vācaspati Miśra's Tattvavaiśāradī develop (Section X) is tracking something real about sustained attention's developmental structure, rather than being merely an internally coherent but empirically unconstrained traditional taxonomy.

Closing Note on This Block

Returning the Expansion to the Paper's Own Centre

Sections XXXVII through XLIV return the expansion programme to Part Six's own proper subject-matter after the prior block's excursion into the wider Vedāṅga survey. Together the eight sections deepen, in turn: the material-causal mechanism (triguṇa) underlying every prior reference to sattva-purification; the practical, parallel discipline (kriyā-yoga) that operates throughout rather than merely preceding the aṣṭāṅga curriculum; the precise psychological mechanism (karmāśaya's kleśa-rootedness) connecting saṃskāra-dynamics to soteriology; the devotional alternative path (Īśvara-praṇidhāna) the system itself recognises as independently sufficient; the older, more pluralist vocabulary (the Gītā's plural yogas) against which Pātañjali's systematisation can be read in relief; the later technical elaboration (Haṭha Yoga) that partially answers the body's relative absence from the root text; the non-dual recognition-model (Kashmir Śaiva re-reading) that refines the series' own central rasāsvādana-argument; and finally an external, empirical check (contemporary neuroscience) on the samāpatti-ladder's claimed phenomenological structure. Taken together with Sections XXIX–XXXVI, this completes a two-block, sixteen-section expansion of the original twelve-section paper.
